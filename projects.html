Exploring Dark Matter Interactions w. SM Particles

(March 2022-Present) | Washington College

Advisor : Dr. Digesh Raut

We know dark matter is out there, but how much of it exists? And why? This project aims to explore properties of dark matter and its interactions with standard model particles using computer-based methods.

This project involved studying relevant literature and particle physics and cosmology research to understand parameters that govern the abundance of dark matter in the universe. So far, I have worked on using Mathematica to solve the Boltzmann Equation for a toy model of dark matter.

This project was student-initiated. It is currently funded by grants from the Cater Society of Junior Fellows and the John S. Toll Summer Student Research Program.

Converting CMS OpenData to 2016 NanoAOD

(May 2022-August 2022) | US CMS @ Fermilab

Advisors : Dr. Nick Smith, Dr. Oksana Shadura

Publicly released CMS OpenData makes it possible for students and hobbyists to work on and explore real data from the CMS Detector at CERN. However, all this data is in the outdated MiniAOD format. This project aims to work on a method to convert ROOT files in the outdated MiniAOD format to 2016 NanoAOD data. The newer datatype is easier to store, understand and process.

This project involved using ROOT, C++ and Python with Coffea-Casa to convert the data and compare the behaviour of the converted file to a sample NanoAOD file. I modified and debugged code, and generated histograms to ensure the converted file was of high enough quality. I had to understand the rough structure of the conversion modules, and studied relevant detector processes to understand the code better. After converting the file, I used Coffea-Casa to make plots comparing the converted 2015 NanoAOD output with the 2016 NanoAOD file.

The output files generated by this process will help ensure high quality, easy to process files are accessible to students and hobbyists working with CMS OpenData.

Modelling Top-Quark Background for LHC Di-Higgs Search

(May 2021-July 2021) | Washington College

Advisor : Dr. Suyog Shrestha

The elusive Higgs Boson! It has been a decade since the Higgs was discovered in 2012, and yet, little remains known about its properties. Di-Higgs collisions, i.e., detector events involving two Higgs Bosons are one of the experimental probes of Higgs properties. These collision signals, hovewer, often suffer from a lot of noise due to the top-quark decay background signal. This project involved simulating the top-quark background signal.

For this project, I worked on running simulations using ROOT and C++ and used real data from the ATLAS detector at the LHC at CERN, Geneva. I plotted and analysed the given data using ROOT and C++, and also learnt to use the Linux operating system. I had to study theory about Higgs properties, particle interactions and detectors.

It was my first research experience, and I had to learn a lot of skills in a limited amount of time (I was just a freshman at the time!) This project was funded by a grant from the John S. Toll Summer Student Research Program at Washington College.
